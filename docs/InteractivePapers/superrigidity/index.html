Margulis Superrigidity

A rigorous overview of the mathematical foundations and core steps of Margulis's Superrigidity Theorem.

Irreducible Lattices and High Rank

The theorem considers an Irreducible Lattice $\Gamma$ in a connected semi-simple algebraic group $G$ without compact factors. A primary example is $\Gamma = SL(n, \mathbb{Z})$ inside $G = SL(n, \mathbb{R})$.

The critical requirement is the **High Rank Condition**: the real rank of $G$ must be at least 2. In rank 1 (such as in $SL(2, \mathbb{R})$ or $SO(n, 1)$), lattices can be "deformed" non-trivially. In higher rank, the algebraic structure becomes rigid.

The Normal Subgroup Theorem: A profound discovery by Margulis; it states that any normal subgroup $N \trianglelefteq \Gamma$ is either finite (contained in the center) or has finite index in $\Gamma$, showcasing the "near-simplicity" of these lattices.

The Furstenberg Boundary ($G/P$)

Margulis's proof relies on the Furstenberg Boundary $B(G)$, which is the quotient $G/P$ where $P$ is a minimal parabolic subgroup (the Borel subgroup). For $SL(n, \mathbb{R})$, this is the variety of full flags in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

The boundary serves as the "proximal" limit where $G$-actions are most descriptive. Because $P$ is an amenable subgroup, we can utilize measure-theoretic tools that aren't available for the group $G$ itself.

Amenability and Boundary Maps: Since $\Gamma$ acts on $B(G)$ and the target boundary $B(H)$, the amenability of $P$ guarantees the existence of a measurable map $\phi: B(G) \to \text{Prob}(B(H))$, providing a crucial bridge between the two geometries.

The Extension Theorem

The core of the proof constructs a **$\Gamma$-equivariant measurable map** $\phi: B(G) \to B(H)$. Using the high-rank geometry (and specifically the fact that the action of $\Gamma$ is "too chaotic" for $\phi$ to be complex), Margulis proves that $\phi$ must be a rational map.

Superrigidity Statement: Let $G$ be a simple algebraic group of $\mathbb{R}\text{-rank} \ge 2$. Any linear representation $\pi: \Gamma \to GL(n, \mathbf{k})$ with unbounded image (where $\mathbf{k}$ is a local field) extends to a rational representation $\tilde{\pi}: G \to GL(n, \mathbf{k})$.

This implies that the representation of the discrete group $\Gamma$ is entirely determined by the continuous structure of the surrounding group $G$.
