Margulis Spacetimes

Drumm's Crooked Plane Construction

Affine Actions on Minkowski Space

Minkowski space $\R^{2,1}$ is $\R^3$ equipped with the Lorentzian inner product

$$\langle v, w \rangle = v_1 w_1 + v_2 w_2 - v_3 w_3$$

An affine Lorentzian isometry is a map $\gamma: v \mapsto Av + b$ where $A \in \SO(2,1)$ and $b \in \R^3$.

Margulis (1983) proved that free groups can act properly discontinuously on $\R^{2,1}$ by affine isometries — the first examples of complete flat Lorentzian manifolds with free fundamental group.

Crooked Planes

Drumm (1990) gave a geometric construction using crooked planes. A crooked plane $C(v, p)$ is determined by:

  • A direction vector $v$, a unit spacelike vector
  • A vertex $p \in \R^{2,1}$

It consists of three pieces:

  1. The stem: $\{ q \in p + v^\perp : \langle q - p, q - p \rangle \leq 0 \}$
  2. Two wings: half-planes tangent to the light cone at $p$, bounded by the two null lines through $p$ in $v^\perp$

A crooked plane is a piecewise-linear surface that "bends" along the light cone at $p$.

Crooked Fundamental Domain

For a proper action of $F_2 = \langle \gamma_1, \gamma_2 \rangle$ on $\R^{2,1}$, Drumm constructs a crooked fundamental domain:

Four crooked planes $C_1^+, C_1^-, C_2^+, C_2^-$ bound a connected region $\mathcal{F}$ such that:

  • $\gamma_1(C_1^-) = C_1^+$ and $\gamma_2(C_2^-) = C_2^+$
  • The crooked planes are pairwise disjoint
  • $\mathcal{F}$ tiles $\R^{2,1}$ under the group action

This is the Lorentzian analogue of a Schottky construction!

The Margulis Invariant

For a hyperbolic element $\gamma$ with linear part $A \in \SO(2,1)$, let $v_\gamma^+, v_\gamma^-, v_\gamma^0$ be the eigenvectors for eigenvalues $\lambda > 1$, $\lambda^{-1}$, and $1$ respectively.

The Margulis invariant is:

$$\alpha(\gamma) = \langle b_\gamma,\, v_\gamma^0 \rangle$$

where $b_\gamma$ is the translational part. This is a class function on conjugacy classes and determines whether the action is proper.

Opposite Sign Lemma (Margulis): A necessary condition for properness is that $\alpha(\gamma_1)$ and $\alpha(\gamma_2)$ have the same sign.

Linear Parts: $\mathrm{Ad}(\mathrm{SL}(2,\Z))$

We use the adjoint representation $\mathrm{Ad}: \mathrm{SL}(2,\Z) \to \SO(2,1)$, acting on $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\R)$ by conjugation $X \mapsto g X g^{-1}$, in the orthogonal basis $$u_1 = E + F = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix},\; u_2 = H = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix},\; u_3 = E-F = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$ where the trace form $B(X,Y) = \mathrm{tr}(XY)$ gives $\mathrm{diag}(2, 2, -2) \sim \mathrm{diag}(1,1,-1)$.

Starting from the $\mathrm{SL}(2,\Z)$ generators:

$$g_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad g_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = g_1^T$$

both hyperbolic with $\mathrm{tr}(g_i) = 3$ and eigenvalues $\phi^{\pm 1}$ where $\phi = \tfrac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$, the adjoint gives $A_i = \mathrm{Ad}(g_i) \in \SO_0(2,1)$:

$$A_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -2 & 3 \\ -2 & 6 & -6 \\ 3 & -6 & 7 \end{pmatrix}, \quad A_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 2 & -3 \\ 2 & 6 & -6 \\ -3 & -6 & 7 \end{pmatrix}$$

with eigenvalues $\{\phi^4, 1, \phi^{-4}\}$ (where $\phi^4 \approx 6.85$). The choice $g_2 = g_1^T$ gives $A_2$ that differs from $A_1$ only by sign flips in the off-diagonal entries involving the first coordinate — a reflection in the $x_2$ direction.

Translation Vectors

The neutral eigenvector $v^0$ (eigenvalue $1$) of $\mathrm{Ad}(g)$ corresponds to the unique element $X \in \mathfrak{sl}(2,\R)$ (up to scale) satisfying $gXg^{-1} = X$, i.e., the centralizer of $g$ in $\mathfrak{sl}(2)$.

For $g_1$: solving $g_1 X = X g_1$ with $X = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} x & y \\ z & -x \end{smallmatrix}\right)$ gives $y = 2x$, $z = 2x$, so

$$X_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = 2 u_1 + 1\cdot u_2 + 0\cdot u_3 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; v_1^0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(2, 1, 0)$$

For $g_2 = g_1^T$: solving $g_2 X = X g_2$ gives $y = -2x$, $z = -2x$, so

$$X_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = -2 u_1 + 1\cdot u_2 + 0\cdot u_3 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; v_2^0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(- 2, 1, 0)$$

Both are spacelike: $\langle v_i^0, v_i^0 \rangle = \tfrac{4+1}{5} = 1 > 0$.

We set the translational parts proportional to these neutral eigenvectors:

$$b_1 = \frac{s}{\sqrt{5}}(2,\, 1,\, 0), \qquad b_2 = \frac{s}{\sqrt{5}}(-2,\, 1,\, 0)$$

where $s > 0$ is the Translation Magnitude slider. The Margulis invariants are then equal:

$$\alpha(\gamma_1) = \langle b_1, v_1^0 \rangle = s, \qquad \alpha(\gamma_2) = \langle b_2, v_2^0 \rangle = s$$

Both positive, confirming properness of the $F_2$-action on $\R^{2,1}$. The affine isometries are:

$$\gamma_i: v \mapsto A_i v + b_i$$
