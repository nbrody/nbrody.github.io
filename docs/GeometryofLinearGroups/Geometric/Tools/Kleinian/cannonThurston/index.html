Quasifuchsian Limit Sets & the Cannon–Thurston Map

e

What you are looking at

For a once–punctured torus, a pair \(A, B \in \mathsf{SL}_2(\mathbb{C})\) with \(\operatorname{tr}[A,B] = -2\) (parabolic commutator) defines a Kleinian representation of \(\pi_1(\Sigma_{1,1})\). Such representations sit inside quasifuchsian space \(\mathrm{QF}(\Sigma_{1,1})\), parameterized (away from a discriminant) by the complex traces \((t_a, t_b, t_{ab})\) subject to the Markov equation \( t_a^2 + t_b^2 + t_{ab}^2 = t_a t_b t_{ab}\).

We use the explicit recipe \( A = \begin{pmatrix} t_a & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix},\ B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \beta \\ -1/\beta & t_b \end{pmatrix}\), where \(\beta + 1/\beta = t_{ab}\). At the Fuchsian point \((t_a,t_b,t_{ab})=(3,3,6)\) the matrices are real and the limit set is a round circle. As \((t_a, t_b)\) deforms into \(\mathbb{C}\), the limit set becomes a quasicircle; approaching the boundary of \(\mathrm{QF}\) it pinches at cusps (rational pleating rays), and in the geometrically infinite direction the quasicircle fills the sphere — the Jordan curve becomes the image of a continuous surjection \( \hat{\mathbb R} \twoheadrightarrow \hat{\mathbb C}\), the Cannon–Thurston map (Cannon–Thurston 1985/2007, McMullen 2001).

Cannon–Thurston coloring: each orbit point \(w\cdot p_0\) is paired with the same word \(w\) applied in the Fuchsian representation \((3,3,6)\). The Fuchsian orbit lives on \(\hat{\mathbb R}\); its angle under the Cayley transform \(x \mapsto (x-i)/(x+i)\) provides a hue. In quasifuchsian pictures this draws the parametrization of the limit quasicircle by \(S^1\); as you move to the boundary, nearby hues smear and the coloring becomes space-filling.
