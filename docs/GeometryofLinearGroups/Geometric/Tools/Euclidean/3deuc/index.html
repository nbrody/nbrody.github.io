What are the possible symmetries of a three-dimensional pattern?

Just as we classified the discrete subgroups of $\Isom(\R^2)$ — point groups, frieze groups, and wallpaper groups — we now tackle the far richer world of $\Isom(\R^3)$.

$$\Isom(\R^3) \cong \R^3 \rtimes \OO(3)$$

The same semidirect product structure, now with $\OO(3)$ — the $3 \times 3$ orthogonal group.

Chapter 1

The Rotation Group $\SO(3)$

Every orientation-preserving isometry of $\R^3$ that fixes the origin is a rotation about some axis through the origin.

  • $\SO(3) = \{ A \in \GL_3(\R) : A^TA = I,\; \det A = 1 \}$
  • Every $A \in \SO(3)$ has an eigenvalue $1$ — its rotation axis
  • The other eigenvalues are $e^{\pm i\theta}$ — the rotation angle

The finite subgroups of $\SO(3)$ have been known since antiquity. They are intimately connected to the Platonic solids.

Chapter 1

Finite Subgroups of $\SO(3)$

Every finite subgroup of $\SO(3)$ is isomorphic to exactly one of:

  • Cyclic $C_n$ — rotations about one axis n ≥ 1
  • Dihedral $D_n$ — $C_n$ plus $n$ half-turns n ≥ 2
  • Tetrahedral $T \cong A_4$ — 12 rotations order 12
  • Octahedral $O \cong S_4$ — 24 rotations order 24
  • Icosahedral $I \cong A_5$ — 60 rotations order 60

Chapter 2

Adding Reflections: $\OO(3)$

The full orthogonal group $\OO(3)$ includes improper isometries: reflections, rotoreflections, and the inversion $x \mapsto -x$.

  • $\OO(3) = \SO(3) \times \{I, -I\}$ when $-I$ is in the group
  • A rotoreflection $\bar{S}_n$: rotate by $2\pi/n$, then reflect through the perpendicular plane
  • The $\bar{S}_2$ rotoreflection is exactly $-I$ (inversion)

The finite subgroups of $\OO(3)$ fall into two families:

  • Type I: subgroups of $\SO(3)$ (rotation-only)
  • Type II: groups containing improper rotations — these extend the Type I groups by adding mirror planes or inversions
Chapter 3

Crystallographic Point Groups

Not every finite subgroup of $\OO(3)$ can be the point group of a crystal. The crystallographic restriction says:

A rotation in the point group of a lattice must have order 1, 2, 3, 4, or 6.

This leaves exactly 32 crystallographic point groups, organized into 7 crystal systems.

  • Triclinic: $C_1$, $C_i$
  • Monoclinic: $C_2$, $C_s$, $C_{2h}$
  • Orthorhombic: $D_2$, $C_{2v}$, $D_{2h}$
  • Tetragonal: $C_4$, $S_4$, $C_{4h}$, $D_4$, $C_{4v}$, $D_{2d}$, $D_{4h}$
  • Trigonal: $C_3$, $S_6$, $D_3$, $C_{3v}$, $D_{3d}$
  • Hexagonal: $C_6$, $C_{3h}$, $C_{6h}$, $D_6$, $C_{6v}$, $D_{3h}$, $D_{6h}$
  • Cubic: $T$, $T_h$, $O$, $T_d$, $O_h$
Chapter 4

Bravais Lattices

The translation subgroup of a 3D crystallographic group is a lattice $\Lambda \cong \Z^3$. Up to symmetry, there are exactly 14 Bravais lattices.

  • Triclinic: 1 lattice (P)
  • Monoclinic: 2 lattices (P, C)
  • Orthorhombic: 4 lattices (P, C, I, F)
  • Tetragonal: 2 lattices (P, I)
  • Trigonal: 1 lattice (R)
  • Hexagonal: 1 lattice (P)
  • Cubic: 3 lattices (P, I, F)

Chapter 5

Space Groups & Bieberbach

A space group (or crystallographic group) is a discrete subgroup $\Gamma \le \Isom(\R^3)$ whose translation subgroup is a rank-3 lattice.

$$1 \to \Lambda \to \Gamma \to G \to 1$$

where $\Lambda \cong \Z^3$ and $G$ is a crystallographic point group.

  • There are exactly 230 space groups in 3D
  • 73 are symmorphic (semidirect product $\Lambda \rtimes G$)
  • 157 are non-symmorphic (involve screw axes or glide planes)

Bieberbach's Theorems (1911)

  • Theorem 1: In any dimension $n$, every discrete cocompact subgroup of $\Isom(\R^n)$ contains a finite-index translation subgroup $\cong \Z^n$.
  • Theorem 2: Two crystallographic groups in $\R^n$ are isomorphic iff they are conjugate by an affine map.
  • Theorem 3: In every dimension, there are only finitely many crystallographic groups (up to isomorphism).
