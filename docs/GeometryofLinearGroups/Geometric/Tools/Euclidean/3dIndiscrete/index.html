Introduction

Indiscrete Isometries
& Property $\FAb$

What happens when we replace $\Z$ with $\Z[1/m]$ in the isometry group of $\R^n$?

Recall that the discrete subgroups of $\Isom(\R^n)$ — the crystallographic groups — are classified by Bieberbach's theorem. But $\Isom_n(\Z[1/m])$ is dense in $\Isom_n(\R)$. These are S-arithmetic groups: not discrete in their real Lie groups, but with rich algebraic structure.

$$\Isom_n(R) \;=\; R^n \rtimes \OO_n(R), \quad R = \Z[1/m]$$

Chapter 1

The Ring $\Z[1/m]$

For a positive integer $m$, the ring $\Z[1/m]$ consists of all fractions whose denominators are powers of $m$:

$$\Z[1/m] = \left\{ \frac{a}{m^k} : a \in \Z,\; k \ge 0 \right\}$$
  • $\Z[1/m]$ is dense in $\R$ — it's indiscrete as a subgroup of $(\R, +)$
  • But it's a principal ideal domain with tractable arithmetic
  • Geometrically: $\Z[1/m]^n$ fills $\R^n$ densely, like a fractal grid

Key example: For $m = 65 = 5 \cdot 13$, we have $\Z[1/65] = \Z[1/5] \cap \Z[1/13]$ in $\Q$. This lets us study the group simultaneously at the primes 5 and 13.

The Group $\Isom_n(R)$

An isometry of $\R^n$ with entries in a ring $R$ is a map $x \mapsto Ax + v$ where $A \in \OO_n(R)$ and $v \in R^n$.

$$\Isom_n(R) = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} A & v \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} : A \in \OO_n(R),\; v \in R^n \right\}$$

  • $\OO_n(R) = \{ A \in \GL_n(R) : A^T A = I_n \}$ acts on $R^n$ by matrix multiplication
  • When $R = \Z$, we recover the crystallographic groups (after taking a discrete subgroup)
  • When $R = \Z[1/m]$, the group is dense in $\Isom_n(\R)$ and infinitely generated over $\Z$
Chapter 2

The Orthogonal Group $\OO_n(\Z[1/m])$

Unlike $\OO_n(\Z)$ (which is finite — just signed permutation matrices), the group $\OO_n(\Z[1/m])$ is infinite.

  • $\OO_n(\Z) = $ signed permutation matrices, $|\OO_n(\Z)| = 2^n \cdot n!$
  • $\OO_n(\Z[1/m])$ is infinite and dense in $\OO_n(\R)$
  • It contains "rational rotations" — rotations by angles whose sine and cosine lie in $\Z[1/m]$
Key Structure

$\OO_n(\Z[1/m])$ can be studied $p$-adically. For each prime $p \mid m$, completion gives $\OO_n(\Zp)$, and we have a diagonal embedding:

$$\OO_n(\Z[1/m]) \hookrightarrow \OO_n(\R) \times \prod_{p \mid m} \OO_n(\Qp)$$

This is the S-arithmetic perspective. The image is a lattice in the product.

Chapter 3

Property $\FAb$

A group $\Gamma$ has Property $\FAb$ if every finite-index subgroup has finite abelianization:

$$\Gamma \text{ has } \FAb \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; [\Gamma : \Delta] < \infty \implies \Delta^{\Ab} \text{ is finite}$$

where $\Delta^{\Ab} = \Delta / [\Delta, \Delta]$ is the abelianization.

Why does this matter?

  • $\FAb$ is a strong rigidity property — it says the group has very few abelian quotients
  • It is implied by Kazhdan's Property (T), but strictly weaker
  • For S-arithmetic groups, $\FAb$ is related to the congruence subgroup property
  • $\FAb$ implies $\Gamma$ cannot surject onto $\Z$ — every homomorphism $\Gamma \to \Z$ is trivial on a finite-index subgroup

Contrast: $\Z^n$ does NOT have $\FAb$ (it is its own abelianization!). Free groups don't either. But higher-rank lattices typically do.

Chapter 4

The Main Theorem

Does $\Isom_3(\Z[1/65])$ have Property $\FAb$?
Theorem

The group $\Isom_3(\Z[1/65])$, where $65 = 5 \cdot 13$, has Property $\FAb$. That is: every finite-index subgroup of $\Isom_3(\Z[1/65])$ has finite abelianization.

Why $m = 65$?

  • $65 = 5 \cdot 13$: we need at least two primes to get "higher rank" in the S-arithmetic sense
  • The primes 5 and 13 are chosen because $\OO_3(\Z[1/p])$ has enough structure at these primes
  • With a single prime $p$, the group $\Isom_3(\Z[1/p])$ is "rank one" over $\Qp$ and does not have $\FAb$
  • Two primes give a lattice in a higher-rank product, enabling rigidity
Chapter 4

Proof Ideas

The proof has two main ingredients:

  • S-arithmetic lattice theorem: Via the diagonal embedding $\Isom_3(\Z[1/65]) \hookrightarrow \Isom_3(\R) \times \Isom_3(\Q_5) \times \Isom_3(\Q_{13})$, the group is a lattice in a locally compact group.
  • Higher rank → rigidity: Since $|S| = \{5, 13\}$ has two primes, the ambient product has higher rank. By results extending Margulis' normal subgroup theorem, lattices in higher-rank products satisfy strong rigidity.
  • The congruence subgroup property: Every finite-index subgroup contains a congruence subgroup $\ker(\Isom_3(\Z[1/65]) \to \Isom_3(\Z/N\Z))$ for some $N$. Congruence quotients are finite, so abelianizations are controlled.
The S-arithmetic viewpoint

The key insight is that $\Isom_n(\Z[1/m])$ is not just a dense subgroup of $\Isom_n(\R)$ — it is simultaneously a discrete lattice when we include the $p$-adic completions. This "recaptures" the discreteness in a higher-dimensional product.
