Chapter 1

Quaternions & $p$-adic Trees

The Hamilton quaternions $\H = \R + \R\bi + \R\bj + \R\bk$ are the unique 4-dimensional division algebra over $\R$. Inside them live the Lipschitz integers $\Z[\bi,\bj,\bk]$ — quaternions with integer coordinates.

How does the arithmetic of integer quaternions control the geometry of $p$-adic trees and $\SO_3$?

For each odd prime $p$, the quaternions of norm $p$ give exactly $p+1$ generators of $\SO_3(\Z[1/p])$. These generators act on the Bruhat–Tits tree $T_p$ — a $(p+1)$-regular tree — and the relations between them tile a square complex.

Chapter 1 · The Finite Group

$\SO_3(\Z) \cong S_4$

The group $\SO_3(\Z)$ consists of all $3\times 3$ integer matrices with determinant $1$ that preserve the standard inner product. It is isomorphic to the symmetric group $S_4$ and has exactly 24 elements.

Via the quaternion representation, each element of $\SO_3(\Z)$ corresponds to a projective integer quaternion of norm $1$, $2$, or $4$, acting by conjugation: $v \mapsto q v \bar{q} / |q|^2$.

Key point: $\SO_3(\Z)$ is the stabilizer of a vertex in the Bruhat–Tits tree. As we invert primes, the group grows — $\SO_3(\Z[1/p])$ acts on the whole tree $T_p$.

Chapter 2 · Prime Generators

The $p+1$ Generators

For each odd prime $p$, the four-square theorem guarantees integer solutions to $a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 = p$. Each such quaternion $q = a + b\bi + c\bj + d\bk$ defines a generator of $\SO_3(\Z[1/p])$.

Projectively (identifying $q$ with $-q$), there are exactly $p+1$ distinct generators. They biject with the projective line $\PP^1(\F_p)$.

Chapter 3 · The Square Complex

Presentation Complex

For two primes $p$ and $q$, every product of a norm-$p$ generator with a norm-$q$ generator can be rewritten:

$\alpha_i \cdot \beta_j = \beta_{j'} \cdot \alpha_{i'}$

where $\alpha_i$ ranges over $p+1$ generators and $\beta_j$ ranges over $q+1$ generators. This gives $(p+1)(q+1)$ commutation relations, each realized as a square (2-cell) in the presentation complex.

Redundancy

When $\alpha_i \cdot \beta_j = \beta_{j'} \cdot \alpha_{i'}$ with $i = i'$ and $j = j'$, the generators commute — these are degenerate squares. We handle them separately and visualize only the non-degenerate $(p+1)(q+1) - d$ squares, where $d$ counts redundancies.

Chapter 3 · The Cayley Graph

Interactive Cayley Graph

The Cayley graph of $\SO_3(\Z[1/n])$ with respect to the chosen generators embeds naturally on the 2-sphere $S^2$ via the conjugation action on pure quaternions.

Each vertex is a group element $g$, placed at $g(\bk) \in S^2$. Edges connect $g$ to $g \cdot \alpha_i$ for each generator $\alpha_i$.

Chapter 4 · Factorization

The Factor Complex

Every integer quaternion $q$ with $|q|^2 = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_k^{e_k}$ can be factored into a product of prime quaternions. The factorization lattice is the directed graph of all such factorizations.

Chapter 5 · $p$-adic Trees

The Bruhat–Tits Tree

The Bruhat–Tits tree $T_p$ is the $(p+1)$-regular tree on which $\PGL_2(\Q_p)$ acts. Each vertex represents a homothety class of $\Z_p$-lattices in $\Q_p^2$.

A quaternion $q$ of norm $p^k$ translates along a geodesic in $T_p$ by distance $k$. The path is determined by the successive $\PP^1(\F_p)$-labels of its prime factors.
