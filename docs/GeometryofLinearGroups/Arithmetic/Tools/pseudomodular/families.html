Hyperbolic Jigsaws
and Families of Pseudomodular Groups

After B. Lou, S.P. Tan, A.D. Vo — arXiv:1611.02922

There are infinitely many commensurability classes of pseudomodular groups — Fuchsian groups whose cusp set is all of $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$ but which are not commensurable to $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$. We explain how jigsaw groups are constructed and how killer intervals prove their pseudomodularity.

1 Background: Pseudomodular Groups

The modular group $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ acts on the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H}^2$ by Möbius transformations. Its cusp set — the orbit of $\infty$ — is precisely $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$. A natural question, posed by Long and Reid, is:

Are there other finitely generated Fuchsian groups, not commensurable with $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$, whose cusp set is still all of $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$?

Such groups are called pseudomodular. Long and Reid (2002) found the first examples — the family $\Delta(u^2, 2\tau)$ — by a delicate computer-assisted search. But their methods left open whether infinitely many commensurability classes exist.

Definition

A pseudomodular group is a discrete subgroup $\Gamma \le \mathrm{PGL}(2,\mathbb{Q})$ such that:

  1. $\Gamma$ is not commensurable with $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$, and
  2. The cusp set of $\Gamma$ is $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$.

The key insight of Lou, Tan, and Vo is that one can systematically construct such groups by assembling fundamental domains from marked ideal triangles — a method they call hyperbolic jigsaws.

2 Jigsaw Tiles

The building block is the jigsaw tile: an ideal triangle in $\mathbb{H}^2$ equipped with extra structure.

Definition (Jigsaw Tile)

A jigsaw tile $\Delta(k_1, k_2, k_3)$ is a rational, balanced, marked ideal triangle:

  • Ideal: All three vertices lie on $\partial\mathbb{H}^2 = \mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\}$.
  • Marked: Each side $s_i$ carries a positive rational parameter $k_i \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}$. One vertex on each side is distinguished (the "marked point").
  • Balanced: $k_1 \cdot k_2 \cdot k_3 = 1$.
  • Rational: $k_1, k_2, k_3 \in \mathbb{Q}$.

Figure 1. A jigsaw tile $\Delta(k_1, k_2, k_3)$ in $\mathbb{H}^2$. Sides are colored by their parameter $k_i$. The marked vertex on each side determines the shear when gluing.

The parameter $k_i$ on side $s_i$ encodes how neighboring tiles will be sheared when glued along that side. Specifically, when two tiles are joined along matching sides, the shear coordinate along the identification is $\log k_i$.

The balancing condition $k_1 k_2 k_3 = 1$ ensures that walking around the triangle and accumulating shears returns you to where you started — a consistency condition for the resulting surface.

Key Types

Lou–Tan–Vo focus on three fundamental tile types:

  • Type 1: $\Delta(n, n, 1/n^2)$ for an integer $n \ge 2$.
  • Type 2: $\Delta(1/n, 1/n, n^2)$ — the "dual" of Type 1.
  • Type 3: $\Delta(m/n, n/m, 1)$ for coprime integers $m, n$.

Every such tile is automatically balanced since its parameter product equals 1.

3 Gluing Tiles into Jigsaws

A jigsaw is an ideal polygon assembled by gluing tiles along matching sides.

Definition (Matching & Jigsaw)

Two sides $s_i$ of $\Delta$ and $s_j'$ of $\Delta'$ match if $k_i = k_j'$. A jigsaw $J$ is an ideal polygon obtained by gluing a finite set $S$ of tiles along matching interior sides. The result is unique up to isometry.

Figure 2. Two tiles glued along a matching side (shown in green). The shear coordinate along the glued edge is $\log k$. The resulting ideal quadrilateral inherits marked points on its exterior sides.

When you glue two tiles $\Delta(k_1, k_2, k_3)$ and $\Delta'(k_1', k_2', k_3')$ along sides with parameter $k_i = k_j'$, the geometric operation is:

  1. Place both triangles in $\mathbb{H}^2$ with the matching sides coinciding.
  2. Apply a shear of $\log k_i$ along the shared geodesic to align the markings.
  3. The shared side becomes an interior diagonal of the resulting ideal quadrilateral.

Iterating this process — gluing more tiles to the exposed sides — builds up larger ideal polygons: pentagons, hexagons, and beyond. The exterior (unglued) sides retain their parameters and become the sides of the jigsawed polygon.

The $S$-Jigsaw Notation

For an integral jigsaw set $S(n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_s)$ with $1 = n_1 < n_2 < \cdots < n_s$, each $n_i$ determines a pair of tiles (Type 1 and Type 2) that interlock. The resulting jigsaw has $2s$ tiles and forms an ideal $(s+2)$-gon.

4 Jigsaw Groups

Once we have a jigsaw polygon, we obtain a Fuchsian group by side-pairing.

The exterior sides of the jigsaw come in matching pairs (they carry the same parameter $k_i$). Identifying each such pair by a Möbius transformation gives:

  • A complete hyperbolic surface $\mathbb{H}^2/\Gamma$ of finite area.
  • A Fuchsian group $\Gamma$ — the jigsaw group — generated by the side-pairing maps.

Since all tile parameters are rational and the vertices are in $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$, the side-pairing maps lie in $\mathrm{PGL}(2,\mathbb{Q})$, so $\Gamma \le \mathrm{PGL}(2,\mathbb{Q})$.

Theorem (Lou–Tan–Vo, 2018)

Let $S$ be a rational balanced jigsaw set and $\Gamma_S$ the corresponding jigsaw group. Then:

  1. $\Gamma_S$ is a finitely generated Fuchsian group of the first kind.
  2. $\Gamma_S \le \mathrm{PGL}(2,\mathbb{Q})$, so every cusp is rational.
  3. If the cusp set equals $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$ and $\Gamma_S$ is not commensurable to $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$, then $\Gamma_S$ is pseudomodular.

The challenge is proving that the cusp set is all of $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$, not just a proper subset. This is where killer intervals enter.

5 Killer Intervals

The concept of a killer interval, introduced by Long and Reid, is the key mechanism for proving that a group's cusp set is all of $\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$.

Definition (Killer Interval)

Let $\Gamma$ be a Fuchsian group and $\gamma \in \Gamma$ with $\gamma = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, $c \ne 0$. The killer interval of $\gamma$ is an interval $I_\gamma$ around the cusp $a/c$ such that for every $p/q \in I_\gamma$ (in lowest terms):

$$\left|\text{denominator of } \gamma^{-1}(p/q)\right| < |q|$$

That is, $\gamma^{-1}$ strictly reduces the denominator of any rational in $I_\gamma$.

The intuition is beautiful. Think of the denominator of a rational $p/q$ as measuring its "complexity." A killer interval is a region where a group element can kill complexity — reduce the denominator. By iterating, we eventually reduce any rational to $\infty$ (denominator 0), proving it's a cusp.

Explicit Formula

For a group element $\gamma = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ with $\det(\gamma) = 1$ and the stabilizer of $\infty$ generating translations by $\lambda$, the killer interval centered at the cusp $a/c$ has half-width:

$$\text{half-width} = \frac{\lambda}{2c^2}$$

So the killer interval is:

$$I_\gamma = \left(\frac{a}{c} - \frac{\lambda}{2c^2},\ \frac{a}{c} + \frac{\lambda}{2c^2}\right)$$

This is intimately related to the isometric circle of $\gamma$, which has center $-d/c$ and radius $1/|c|$. The width of the killer interval is inversely proportional to $c^2$ — so elements with large $|c|$ (deep in the group) produce narrow intervals.

Figure 3. Killer intervals on the number line. Each semicircle represents an interval around a cusp. The width $\sim 1/q^2$ for a cusp $p/q$ — resembling the Stern–Brocot / Ford circle picture.

6 The Covering Argument

The strategy for proving pseudomodularity is:

Covering Criterion (Long–Reid)

A group $\Gamma$ is pseudomodular if and only if the union of killer intervals of all elements $\gamma \in \Gamma$ covers $\mathbb{R}$. Equivalently, it suffices to cover a single fundamental interval $[0, \lambda]$ (where $\lambda$ is the translation length of the parabolic element stabilizing $\infty$).

The logic is an inductive descent:

  1. Start with any $p/q \in \mathbb{Q}$. By translation, we may assume $p/q \in [0, \lambda]$.
  2. Find a killer interval $I_\gamma$ containing $p/q$.
  3. Apply $\gamma^{-1}$, obtaining $p'/q'$ with $|q'| < |q|$.
  4. Translate $p'/q'$ back into $[0, \lambda]$.
  5. Repeat. The denominator strictly decreases at each step.
  6. Terminate when the denominator reaches 0, meaning $p/q$ has been mapped to $\infty$.

Hence $p/q$ is in the $\Gamma$-orbit of $\infty$, i.e., it is a cusp. Since $p/q$ was arbitrary, $\mathrm{Cusps}(\Gamma) = \mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}$. ∎

Analogy with the Euclidean Algorithm

This is a non-commutative generalization of the Euclidean algorithm. For $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$, the killer interval of the element $T^n S$ (where $T: z\mapsto z+1$ and $S: z\mapsto -1/z$) is the open interval $(n, n+1)$. Together, these intervals tile $\mathbb{R}$, and the descent process is precisely the ordinary continued fraction algorithm. For pseudomodular groups, the intervals overlap and the descent is more intricate — but the principle is the same.

7 Infinite Families

Lou, Tan, and Vo prove that specific families of jigsaw groups are pseudomodular and pairwise non-commensurable.

Main Theorem (Lou–Tan–Vo)

There exist infinitely many commensurability classes of pseudomodular groups. Specifically:

  1. Jigsaws formed from one Type 2 tile and $n$ Type 1 tiles (for any $n \ge 1$) are all pseudomodular.
  2. These jigsaw groups are pairwise non-commensurable.
  3. Hence there are infinitely many commensurability classes of pseudomodular groups and surfaces.

The proof combines:

  • Jigsaw construction to produce explicit fundamental domains and generators in $\mathrm{PGL}(2,\mathbb{Q})$.
  • Killer interval analysis to verify that the fundamental interval $[0, \lambda]$ is covered, establishing pseudomodularity.
  • Invariant trace field computations to distinguish commensurability classes. Different tile parameters yield different trace fields, so the groups cannot be commensurable.

They also show that jigsaws using Type 1 and Type 3 tiles produce both pseudomodular and non-pseudomodular groups, revealing a rich landscape.
