ü™¢ The Braid Group \(B_n\)

The braid group \(B_n\) on \(n\) strands is a fundamental object in topology, algebra, and mathematical physics. It can be thought of as the group of "ways to braid \(n\) strands" up to ambient isotopy.

Presentation

\(B_n\) has generators \(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_{n-1}\) (the elementary braids) subject to the braid relations:

\[ \sigma_i \sigma_j = \sigma_j \sigma_i \quad \text{if } |i - j| \geq 2 \] \[ \sigma_i \sigma_{i+1} \sigma_i = \sigma_{i+1} \sigma_i \sigma_{i+1} \]

The first relation expresses that distant crossings commute, and the second is the braid (Yang‚ÄìBaxter) relation involving adjacent generators.

Geometrically, \(\sigma_i\) crosses strand \(i\) over strand \(i{+}1\). The group is infinite and non-abelian. It surjects onto the symmetric group \(S_n\) via the map \(\sigma_i \mapsto (i\ \ i{+}1)\).

Why study representations of \(B_n\)?

  • The Burau representation is faithful for \(n \leq 3\) and known to be unfaithful for \(n \geq 4\).
  • Finding explicit kernel elements ‚Äî non-trivial braids that act as the identity under Burau ‚Äî is a computationally interesting challenge.
  • Relations in the Burau image correspond to constraints on the topology of the underlying braids.

üìä The Reduced Burau Representation

The reduced Burau representation is a homomorphism \(\psi: B_n \to \mathrm{GL}_{n-1}\!\big(\mathbb{Z}[t, t^{-1}]\big)\), sending each braid to an invertible matrix over the ring of Laurent polynomials in one variable \(t\).

For \(B_n\) ‚Äî \((n{-}1) \times (n{-}1)\) Matrices

The representation maps each generator \(\sigma_i\) to an \((n{-}1)\times (n{-}1)\) matrix. The action on the free module with basis \(e_1, \ldots, e_{n-1}\) is:

\[ \sigma_i(e_j) = e_j \quad \text{if } j \neq i{-}1, i, i{+}1 \] \[ \sigma_i(e_{i-1}) = e_{i-1} + t\, e_i, \quad \sigma_i(e_i) = {-t}\, e_i, \quad \sigma_i(e_{i+1}) = e_i + e_{i+1} \]

Faithfulness and the Kernel

A representation is faithful if its kernel is trivial. Burau showed the representation is faithful for \(n \leq 3\), but Moody (1991) proved it is unfaithful for \(n \geq 9\), and Long‚ÄìPaton and Bigelow pushed this down to \(n \geq 4\). For \(n = 4\), there exist non-trivial braids \(w \in B_4\) with \(\psi(w) = I\).

Finding such elements computationally is the goal of our FlashBeam search.

üíç Quotient Rings

Working in the full Laurent polynomial ring \(\mathbb{Z}[t, t^{-1}]\) leads to coefficient explosion: as words get longer, matrix entries become polynomials of very high degree with huge coefficients.

To make the search tractable, we can specialize to a quotient ring \(\mathbb{Z}[t, t^{-1}] / (p(t))\), where \(p(t)\) is an irreducible polynomial. This forces \(t\) to satisfy the relation \(p(t) = 0\), bounding the degree of all computations.

Key Specializations
  • \(t = 1\): The Burau representation collapses to the permutation representation. The kernel is the pure braid group.
  • \(t = -1\): Related to homology of double covers. Important in knot theory.
  • \(t = \omega\) (root of unity): The image becomes a finite matrix group, making exhaustive search possible.
  • \(t^2 = 2\): Embeds into \(\mathrm{GL}_3(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}])\), an arithmetic group of interest.

Relations found in a quotient ring \(R/(p(t))\) correspond to elements of the braid group that act trivially in the specialized representation. These may or may not be in the kernel of the full Burau representation ‚Äî they are, however, elements of the kernel of the specialized homomorphism.

üå≤ Restricting to Subgroups

To focus the search, we can restrict to subgroups generated by subsets of the generators. For instance (when \(n \geq 4\)):

  • \(\langle \sigma_i, \sigma_j \rangle\) with \(|i - j| \geq 2\): These commute, so this is a free abelian subgroup \(\mathbb{Z}^2\). No non-trivial relations expected in any faithful representation.
  • \(\langle \sigma_i, \sigma_{i+1} \rangle \cong B_3\): The Burau rep of \(B_3\) is faithful, so no kernel here.
  • The full group \(B_n\) with all generators: this is where unfaithfulness is known for \(n \geq 4\).
