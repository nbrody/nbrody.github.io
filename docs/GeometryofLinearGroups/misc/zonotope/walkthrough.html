The Longest Word

Factorizations, tilings, and the symmetric group

The Reversal

Consider the simplest dramatic thing you can do to a sequence: reverse it.

$w_0 : (1, 2, 3, \ldots, n) \;\mapsto\; (n, \ldots, 3, 2, 1)$

This is the longest element $w_0$ of the symmetric group $S_n$. It sends every element as far away as possible from where it started.

Can we build $w_0$ from simpler pieces? The symmetric group is generated by adjacent transpositions:

$s_i \;:\; i \leftrightarrow i\!+\!1$

Factorizing $w_0$

We need to write $w_0$ as a product of adjacent transpositions $s_i$, using as few as possible.

For $n = 4$, the reversal $(1234) \to (4321)$ requires exactly $\binom{n}{2} = 6$ swaps — one for each pair of elements that need to cross.

Here is one way to do it. Read right to left:

s₁ · s₂ · s₁ · s₃ · s₂ · s₁

Each $s_i$ swaps neighbors $i$ and $i\!+\!1$. The arc diagram to the right shows each crossing.

Many Factorizations

There are many reduced words for $w_0$. For $n = 4$, there are 16 of them.

A natural question arises:

Can any two reduced factorizations be connected by local moves?

There are two kinds of local moves on reduced words:

  • Commutation: $s_i s_j = s_j s_i$ when $|i - j| \geq 2$
  • Braid move: $s_i s_{i+1} s_i = s_{i+1} s_i s_{i+1}$

Matsumoto's Theorem: Yes! Any two reduced words for $w_0$ are connected by a sequence of commutation and braid moves.

But what does the space of all such factorizations look like?

Enter: Rhombus Tilings

There is a beautiful bijection between reduced words for $w_0 \in S_n$ and rhombus tilings of the regular $2n$-gon.

Each tiling decomposes the $2n$-gon into $\binom{n}{2}$ rhombi — exactly one for each pair of vectors.

  • Each rhombus corresponds to a transposition $s_i$ in the word
  • Reading order through the tiling gives the factorization
  • A braid move $s_i s_{i+1} s_i \leftrightarrow s_{i+1} s_i s_{i+1}$ corresponds to flipping a cube

Paths in the $n$-Cube

There is another way to see these tilings: as monotone paths across a projected $n$-cube.

The $2n$-gon is the image of the $n$-cube under a projection $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^2$. Each tiling lifts to a monotone path from the "top" vertex $(1,1,\ldots,1)$ to the "bottom" vertex $(0,0,\ldots,0)$.

  • The top tiling corresponds to one extremal path
  • The bottom tiling corresponds to the other
  • All paths are connected by square flips — crossing a 2-face of the cube

The graph of all tilings, connected by flips, is a flip graph — and it is always connected. This is precisely the content of Matsumoto's Theorem, seen geometrically!
