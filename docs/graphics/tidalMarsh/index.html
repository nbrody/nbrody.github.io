Tidal salt marshes — like Bair Island in San Francisco Bay — develop dendritic creek networks through a channelization instability: where ebb-tide flow happens to concentrate along an incipient creek, erosion deepens it, and on the next tide it captures even more flow. Over many cycles this positive feedback breaks a near-uniform tidal flat into the fractal drainage you see from the air (Howard 1971; Smith & Bretherton 1972; for tidal networks Fagherazzi & Sun 2004 and D’Alpaos, Lanzoni, Marani 2007).

The model is the simplest version that captures the essential geometry: stream-power erosion ∝ AmSn coupled with hillslope diffusion (sediment slumping) and a uniform sediment supply U. The same equation governs fluvial landscapes — there U is tectonic uplift; here it stands in for tidal deposition during slack water.

This is not a Turing instability — Turing requires two diffusing fields in activator–inhibitor coupling, and the spatial coupling here is geometric (drainage on a 2D surface), not diffusive. Real marshes also have vegetation feedback and bidirectional tidal flow, both omitted; what survives the simplification is the flow-concentration feedback that is responsible for the network's fractal character.
