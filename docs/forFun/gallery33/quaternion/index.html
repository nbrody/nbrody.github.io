33
a quaternion story

Here's my favorite fact about the number 33.

Its prime factorization is 3 × 11.

33 = 3 × 11

Sorting the primes

There is only one even prime — 2. Every other prime is odd.

Something interesting happens when you sort them: primes of the form 4n+1 (like 5, 13, 17) and primes of the form 4n+3 (like 3, 7, 11).

A deep result

In a certain sense, half the primes are in each bucket. This is a deep theorem in analytic number theory.

It relates to why 33 is special: it is the smallest number divisible by two distinct primes of the form 8n+3.

3 = 8(0) + 3
11 = 8(1) + 3

Complex numbers

You're probably familiar with the complex numbers: numbers of the form \(a + bi\), where \(i^2 = -1\).

These are 2-dimensional numbers.

The quaternions

There's a fascinating way to iterate the construction from reals to complex numbers, creating a 4-dimensional number system.

These were first studied by Hamilton in 1843.

\( i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1 \)

A strange property

Quaternions do not commute under multiplication. If \(x\) and \(y\) are quaternions, it's not necessarily true that \(xy = yx\).

This makes their factorization theory extremely fascinating!

The norm

A quaternion is \(q = a + bi + cj + dk\), where we'll focus on integer quaternions.

The norm of \(q\) is \(a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2\).

\(1 + i + j\)
\(\downarrow\)
\(1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 + 0^2\)
= 3

Jacobi's theorem

When we multiply two quaternions, their norms multiply.

Jacobi proved in 1834 — before quaternions were even defined! — that the number of ways to write an odd prime \(p\) as a sum of four squares is \(8(p+1)\).

The "8" comes from the 8 unit quaternions: \(\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\). The remaining \(p+1\) come in conjugate pairs.

Conjugate pairs

When we take a quaternion like \(1+i+3j\) and multiply it by its conjugate \(1-i-3j\), we recover the norm.

\((1+i+3j)(1-i-3j)\)
\(= 1^2 + 1^2 + 3^2 + 0^2\)
\(= 11\)

The factorization square

Every quaternion of norm 33 factors as a product of prime quaternions in two ways — with the norm-3 factor on the left, or the norm-11 factor on the left.

Both paths around the square give the same quaternion.

A product of trees

All of this together builds a beautiful space — a product of trees!

The factorization squares tile the product of the Bruhat–Tits trees for \(\mathbb{Q}_3\) and \(\mathbb{Q}_{11}\).
