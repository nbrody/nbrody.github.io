Let's start with an example that contains all of these ideas at once.

Integers Acting on the Line

Consider the real number line \(\R\) — the most basic geometric space.

The integers \(\Z = \{\ldots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) act on \(\R\) by translation:

\[n \cdot x = x + n\]

Each integer shifts the entire line. This is a symmetry — it preserves distances.

The orbit of any point \(x\) is the set of all its translates:

\[\Z \cdot x = \{\ldots, x-2,\; x-1,\; x,\; x+1,\; x+2, \ldots\}\]

Wrapping into a Circle

Now glue together all points in the same orbit — declare \(x \sim y\) whenever \(x - y \in \Z\).

The interval \([0, 1)\) contains exactly one representative from each orbit. Gluing the endpoints \(0 \sim 1\) wraps this interval into a circle.

\[\R \,/\, \Z \;\cong\; S^1\]

Space (the line \(\R\)) + Symmetry (integer translations \(\Z\)) → Quotient (the circle \(S^1\)).
And as we'll see, the paradox lives here too.

Space

What does it mean to measure distance?

Metric Spaces

Mathematics begins with abstraction. We take a familiar idea — distance — and strip it to its essentials.

A metric space is a set \(X\) together with a function \(d\colon X \times X \to [0,\infty)\) satisfying:

  1. \(d(x,y) = 0\) if and only if \(x = y\)
  2. \(d(x,y) = d(y,x)\)   (symmetry)
  3. \(d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z)\)   (triangle inequality)

That's it! Any set with a "distance function" satisfying these three rules is a metric space.

The Number Line

\(d(x,y) = |x - y|\)

\(d = \sqrt{(x_1-x_2)^2 + (y_1-y_2)^2}\)

Trees & Graphs

Shortest path length between vertices

Trees

A tree is a graph with no cycles — there is exactly one path between any two vertices.

The distance between two vertices is the number of edges in this unique path.

Trees look very different from the line or the plane, but they satisfy all three metric space axioms perfectly.

Trees are the hidden geometry behind free groups. They will turn out to be the key to the Banach–Tarski paradox.

Symmetry

Rigid motions and group actions

Rigid Motions

What are the symmetries of a metric space?

An isometry of a metric space \((X, d)\) is a bijection \(f\colon X \to X\) that preserves distances:

\[d\big(f(x),\, f(y)\big) = d(x, y) \quad \text{for all } x,y \in X\]

Isometries are the rigid motions — the ways to move a space without distorting it.

Groups

The set of all isometries of a space forms a group, written \(\Isom(X)\).

A group \(G\) is a set with a binary operation \(\cdot\) satisfying:

  1. Associativity: \((a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)\)
  2. Identity: there exists \(e\) with \(e \cdot g = g \cdot e = g\)
  3. Inverses: for each \(g\), there exists \(g^{-1}\) with \(g \cdot g^{-1} = e\)

Every symmetry can be composed with another, undone, or left alone. That's exactly what a group captures.

Group Actions

When a group \(G\) acts on a space \(X\), each element \(g \in G\) moves points around.

For each point \(x \in X\), the orbit of \(x\) is the set of all points you can reach:

\[G \cdot x = \{g \cdot x \mid g \in G\}\]

The quotient space \(X / G\) is formed by collapsing each orbit to a single point — the space of "essentially different" positions.

Symmetry Groups

  • The line \(\R\): translations \(x \mapsto x + t\) and the reflection \(x \mapsto -x\).   \(\Isom(\R) \cong \R \rtimes \Z/2\)
  • The plane \(\R^2\): translations, rotations, reflections, and glide reflections
  • The sphere \(S^2\): rotations and reflections.   \(\Isom(S^2) = \OO(3)\)
  • A \(k\)-regular tree: enormously symmetric! The isometry group is uncountably infinite.

More symmetric spaces have larger isometry groups.

Quotients

When symmetries act, new shapes emerge

\(\R \,/\, \Z \;\cong\; S^1\)

The integers \(\Z\) act on the line by translation \(x \mapsto x + n\).

Gluing together points that differ by an integer wraps the line into a circle.

\[0 \sim 1 \sim 2 \sim \cdots \qquad \tfrac{1}{2} \sim \tfrac{3}{2} \sim \tfrac{5}{2} \sim \cdots\]

The fundamental domain is the interval \([0, 1)\) — one representative from each orbit.

\(\R^2 / \Z^2 \;\cong\; T^2\)

The integer lattice \(\Z^2\) acts on the plane by translation.

Gluing opposite edges of a unit square gives a torus — a donut shape!

The fundamental domain is the unit square \([0,1) \times [0,1)\). Glue left↔right, then top↔bottom.

Same recipe: a symmetric group acts on a space, and the quotient is a new geometric object.

\(\HH^2 / \Delta\)

The hyperbolic plane \(\HH^2\) is a space where triangles have angles summing to less than \(180°\).

A triangle group \(\Delta(p,q,r)\) is generated by reflections in the sides of a hyperbolic triangle with angles \(\pi/p\), \(\pi/q\), \(\pi/r\).

The quotient \(\HH^2 / \Delta\) is a hyperbolic orbifold — a triangle with cone-point singularities at the vertices.

The triangle tiles the entire hyperbolic plane, like an infinite kaleidoscope.

Tree \(\,/\, F_2\)

The free group \(F_2 = \langle a, b \rangle\) acts on the 4-regular tree by isometries.

Each element is a unique "word" in \(a, b, a^{-1}, b^{-1}\) — no relations, no coincidences.

The quotient is a rose — a wedge of two circles, one for each generator.

\[T_4 \,/\, F_2 \;\cong\; S^1 \vee S^1\]

Free groups are trees in disguise. This tree structure will be the engine of the paradox.

\(S^2 / G\)

Finite subgroups of \(\SO(3)\) — the rotation groups of regular solids — act on the sphere.

  • \(S^2 / (\Z/n)\): rotations around one axis → a "lemon" shape with two cone points
  • \(S^2 / \text{(icosahedral)}\): the 60 rotational symmetries of the icosahedron → a sphere with special points
  • \(S^2 / (x \sim -x)\): identify antipodal points → the projective plane \(\R P^2\)

The richer the symmetry group, the smaller the quotient.

Paradox

When infinity breaks the rules

Removing a Point

Consider the natural numbers \(\N = \{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\).

The map \(n \mapsto n + 1\) is a bijection from \(\N\) to \(\N \setminus \{0\}\):

\[0 \mapsto 1, \quad 1 \mapsto 2, \quad 2 \mapsto 3, \quad \ldots\]

We can remove a point from an infinite set without making it any smaller!

This is Hilbert's Hotel: a hotel with infinitely many rooms can always accommodate one more guest — just shift everyone up by one.

Half Equals Whole

Even more dramatic: the map \(n \mapsto 2n\) is a bijection from \(\N\) to the even numbers:

\[0 \mapsto 0, \quad 1 \mapsto 2, \quad 2 \mapsto 4, \quad 3 \mapsto 6, \quad \ldots\]

Half of the natural numbers is "the same size" as all of them.

Infinite sets can be split into pieces, each of which is as big as the whole. This is only paradoxical because our finite intuitions break down.

A Paradox on the Circle

Now let's move from counting to geometry.

Pick an irrational rotation \(\alpha\) of the circle \(S^1\). The orbit of any point \(p\) is a countable set \(\{p,\; \alpha(p),\; \alpha^2(p),\; \ldots\}\) that never repeats.

This orbit looks like \(\N\) sitting inside \(S^1\). Applying Hilbert's trick:

\[\text{Rotate the orbit by } \alpha \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{the orbit shifts, and } p \text{ is freed}\]

By rotating a subset of \(S^1\), we can remove a point from the circle using a rigid motion!

The Free Group Paradox

The free group \(F_2 = \langle a, b \rangle\) takes this much further.

Partition its elements into four pieces based on the first letter of each reduced word:

  • \(W_a\) = words starting with \(a\)
  • \(W_{a^{-1}}\) = words starting with \(a^{-1}\)
  • \(W_b\) = words starting with \(b\)
  • \(W_{b^{-1}}\) = words starting with \(b^{-1}\)

\(a^{-1} \cdot W_a = W_a \cup W_b \cup W_{b^{-1}} \cup \{e\}\)

Multiplying by \(a^{-1}\) "absorbs" the leading \(a\), giving everything else back!

The Banach–Tarski Paradox (1924)

A solid ball in \(\R^3\) can be decomposed into a finite number of pieces which can be reassembled — using only rotations and translations — into two solid balls, each the same size as the original.

Why Isn't This Nonsense?

The pieces are not ordinary geometric shapes — they are non-measurable sets, constructed using the Axiom of Choice.

  • You can never physically perform this decomposition
  • The pieces have no well-defined volume — they're too "fractal-like"
  • This shows that not every subset of \(\R^3\) can be assigned a volume that's preserved by rigid motions

The Banach–Tarski paradox is really a theorem about the richness of the rotation group. The sphere has so many symmetries that free groups hide inside \(\SO(3)\), and free groups can do "paradoxical" things.
