Space

What is space?

Mathematics begins with abstraction. We take a familiar idea — distance — and strip it to its absolute essentials.

A set of points where a distance \(d(x,y)\) satisfies:

  1. \(d(x,y) = 0 \iff x = y\)
  2. \(d(x,y) = d(y,x)\)   (Symmetry)
  3. \(d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z)\)   (Triangle Inequality)

Any set following these rules is a Metric Space.

Rigid Motions

An Isometry is a rearrangement of space that preserves distance. It is a rigid motion — no stretching, no squishing.

Symmetry

What is a Group?

A Group is a mathematical object that captures the idea of "composing symmetries."

  1. Composition: You can combine two symmetries
  2. Identity: "Doing nothing" is a symmetry
  3. Inverse: Every symmetry can be undone

The Circle Quotient

Glue together points that differ by an integer — we wrap the line into a Circle.

\(\R \,/\, \Z \;\cong\; S^1\)

Quotients

How Big is a Million?

In the \(p\)-adic numbers, size is determined by divisibility by a prime \(p\).

Real Size

1,000,000 is huge.

2-adic Size

\(10^6 = 2^6 \cdot 5^6\)
It is divisible by 2 many times.
It is tiny.

This completely inverts our intuition of "far" and "near."

The \(p\)-adic Tree

The \(p\)-adic numbers have a fractal, tree-like geometry. Every rational rotation "shadows" onto one of these trees.

Free Groups

Symmetries of the Tree

A group is Free if there are no coincidences. Every word is a unique path in a tree.

Higher Dimensions

When we combine these symmetries, we get higher-dimensional "product" spaces.

The Paradox

The Vanishing Trick

Consider the natural numbers \(\{0,1,2,3,\dots\}\). Add 1 to every number. We get \(\{1,2,3,\dots\}\) — the same set, but 0 is gone!

The Doubling Trick

Split \(\N\) into evens and odds. Relabel each half — and each gives back a complete copy of \(\N\)! One infinity becomes two.

The Free Group Paradox

Partition the group into words starting with \(a, a^{-1}, b, b^{-1}\).

\(a^{-1} \cdot W_a = W_a \cup W_b \cup W_{b^{-1}} \cup \{e\}\)

Rotating one quarter gives you three quarters back!

Banach–Tarski Paradox

Choose two rotations that are "free." Their orbits partition the sphere into pieces that can double.
