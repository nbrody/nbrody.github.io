Metric Spaces

What does it mean to measure distance?

Measuring Distance

Mathematics begins with abstraction. We take a familiar idea — distance — and strip it to its essentials.

A metric space is a set \(X\) together with a function \(d\colon X \times X \to [0,\infty)\) satisfying:

  1. \(d(x,y) = 0\) if and only if \(x = y\)
  2. \(d(x,y) = d(y,x)\)   (symmetry)
  3. \(d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z)\)   (triangle inequality)

That's it! Any set with a "distance function" satisfying these three rules is a metric space.

Examples

📏

The Number Line

\(d(x,y) = |x - y|\)

📐

The Plane

\(d = \sqrt{(x_1-x_2)^2 + (y_1-y_2)^2}\)

The Sphere

Rigid Motions

Now for the key question: what are the symmetries of a metric space?

An isometry of a metric space \((X, d)\) is a bijection \(f\colon X \to X\) that preserves distances:

\[d\big(f(x),\, f(y)\big) = d(x, y) \quad \text{for all } x,y \in X\]

Isometries are the rigid motions — the ways to move a space without distorting it.

The set of all isometries of \(X\) forms a group, written \(\Isom(X)\).

Isometry Groups

  • The line \(\R\): translations \(x \mapsto x + t\) and the reflection \(x \mapsto -x\).   \(\Isom(\R) \cong \R \rtimes \Z/2\)
  • The plane \(\R^2\): translations, rotations, reflections, and glide reflections
  • The sphere \(S^2\): rotations and reflections.   \(\Isom(S^2) = \OO(3)\)

More symmetric spaces have larger isometry groups. The sphere is very symmetric!

Groups & Quotients

When symmetries act, new shapes emerge

What Is a Group?

A group is an algebraic structure that captures the idea of symmetry.

A group \(G\) is a set with a binary operation \(\cdot\) satisfying:

  1. Associativity: \((a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)\)
  2. Identity: there exists \(e\) with \(e \cdot g = g \cdot e = g\)
  3. Inverses: for each \(g\), there exists \(g^{-1}\) with \(g \cdot g^{-1} = e\)

The isometries of any metric space naturally form a group: you can compose motions, undo them, and doing nothing is always an option.

Group Actions

When a group \(G\) acts on a space \(X\), each element \(g \in G\) moves points around.

For each point \(x \in X\), the orbit of \(x\) is the set of all points you can reach:

\[G \cdot x = \{g \cdot x \mid g \in G\}\]

The quotient space \(X / G\) is formed by gluing together each orbit into a single point. It's the space of "essentially different" positions.

Quotient Examples

  • \(\R / \Z\): the integers act on the line by translation. Gluing \(x \sim x+1\) wraps the line into a circle.
  • \(\R^2 / \Z^2\): a lattice of translations turns the plane into a torus (donut shape!).
  • \(S^2 / (x \sim -x)\): identify opposite points on the sphere and you get the projective plane — a one-sided surface.

Quotients let us build new geometric objects from old ones, using group symmetry as the glue.

Rotations of the Sphere

The symmetry group \(\SO(3)\)

The Rotation Group

Every rotation of the sphere is determined by an axis (a line through the center) and an angle of rotation.

The group of all rotations is called \(\SO(3)\) — the special orthogonal group.

It consists of all \(3 \times 3\) matrices \(A\) with:

  • \(A^T A = I\)   (orthogonal — preserves distances)
  • \(\det(A) = 1\)   (no reflections)

Rational Rotations

What if we restrict to rotations whose matrix entries are rational numbers?

The subgroup \(\SO(3, \Q)\) is still infinite, and it's dense in \(\SO(3)\) — any rotation can be approximated arbitrarily well by a rational one.

Rational rotations have entries in \(\Q\), and rational numbers can be studied through the lens of prime numbers. This gives these rotations a rich hidden structure!

An Orbit Mystery

Pick two rotations \(a, b \in \SO(3)\) and consider the group \(\langle a, b \rangle\) they generate.

Now pick a point \(p\) on the sphere. What does its orbit look like?

If \(a\) and \(b\) are "generic enough," the orbit \(\langle a, b \rangle \cdot p\) is a dense subset of the sphere. The orbit fills up the whole sphere!

But how it fills up the sphere is subtle. To understand it, we need a different kind of number system...

Hidden Geometry

The \(p\)-adic world behind rational rotations

The \(p\)-adic Numbers

The real numbers \(\R\) measure size using the usual absolute value: \(1000000\) is big, \(0.000001\) is small.

But there's a completely different way to measure size!

For a prime \(p\), define \(|x|_p\) by writing \(x = p^k \cdot \frac{a}{b}\) where \(p \nmid a, b\). Then:

\[|x|_p = p^{-k}\]

So \(|1000000|_2 = |2^6 \cdot 5^6|_2 = 2^{-6} = \frac{1}{64}\) — a million is tiny to the 2-adics!

A Different World

Completing \(\Q\) with respect to \(|\cdot|_p\) gives the \(p\)-adic numbers \(\Q_p\).

Every absolute value on \(\Q\) is equivalent to either the usual \(|\cdot|\) or some \(|\cdot|_p\). The real numbers and the \(p\)-adic numbers are all the completions of \(\Q\).

The \(p\)-adic numbers have a tree-like geometry: the "unit ball" in \(\Q_p\) splits into \(p\) smaller balls, each of which splits into \(p\) even smaller balls, and so on...

The Hidden Action

Here's the magic: a rational rotation \(A \in \SO(3, \Q)\) acts not only on the round sphere \(S^2 \subset \R^3\), but also on a \(p\)-adic sphere for every prime \(p\)!

\[\SO(3, \Q) \hookrightarrow \SO(3, \R) \times \prod_{p} \SO(3, \Q_p)\]

The action on the real sphere is just ordinary rotation. But the \(p\)-adic actions give extra structure: they act on trees, providing a hidden combinatorial skeleton.

It's like a rotation casting multiple "shadows" — one for each prime number. Each shadow reveals a different aspect of the rotation's nature.

Free Groups and Paradox

A group is called free if its elements have no "unexpected" relations — each product of generators is distinct.

For "most" choices of two rotations \(a, b\), the group \(\langle a, b \rangle\) is a free group on 2 generators.

This means every element can be written uniquely as a reduced word like

\(a \cdot b^{-1} \cdot a \cdot a \cdot b \cdot a^{-1} \cdot b \cdot b\)

The tree-like structure of the \(p\)-adic world is exactly what lets us prove that these groups are free. And free groups are the engine of the Banach–Tarski paradox.

The Banach–Tarski Paradox

One sphere becomes two

A Paradox with the Integers

Before tackling spheres, consider a simpler "paradox."

The integers \(\Z = \{\ldots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) can be split into evens and odds.

But the even integers are "the same size" as all the integers: just multiply by 2! \[\Z \xrightarrow{\;\times 2\;} 2\Z\]

So somehow, half of the integers is "as big as" all of them. This is only paradoxical because infinite sets don't obey our finite intuitions.

Free Group Paradox

The free group \(F_2 = \langle a, b \rangle\) has a much more dramatic version.

Partition the elements of \(F_2\) into four pieces based on the first letter of each reduced word:

  • \(W_a\) = words starting with \(a\)
  • \(W_{a^{-1}}\) = words starting with \(a^{-1}\)
  • \(W_b\) = words starting with \(b\)
  • \(W_{b^{-1}}\) = words starting with \(b^{-1}\)

\(a^{-1} \cdot W_a = W_a \cup W_b \cup W_{b^{-1}} \cup \{e\}\)

Multiplying by \(a^{-1}\) "absorbs" the leading \(a\), giving everything else back!

From Groups to Spheres

Choose two rotations \(a, b \in \SO(3)\) generating a free group. Their orbits partition almost all of the sphere.

Using the free group decomposition, we can split the sphere (minus a countable set) into 4 pieces:

\[S^2 = W_a \cdot p \;\cup\; W_{a^{-1}} \cdot p \;\cup\; W_b \cdot p \;\cup\; W_{b^{-1}} \cdot p \;\cup\; \text{(countable)}\]

Rotating just two of the pieces (by \(a^{-1}\) and \(b^{-1}\)) reconstructs the whole sphere each time!

The Banach–Tarski Paradox (1924)

A solid ball in \(\R^3\) can be decomposed into a finite number of pieces which can be reassembled — using only rotations and translations — into two solid balls, each the same size as the original.

Why Isn't This Nonsense?

The pieces are not ordinary geometric shapes — they are non-measurable sets, constructed using the Axiom of Choice.

  • You can never physically perform this decomposition
  • The pieces have no well-defined volume — they're too "fractal-like"
  • This shows that not every subset of \(\R^3\) can be assigned a volume that's preserved by rigid motions

The Banach–Tarski paradox is really a theorem about the richness of the rotation group. The sphere has so many symmetries that free groups hide inside \(\SO(3)\), and free groups can do "paradoxical" things.

The \(p\)-adic Connection

How does all this connect back to \(p\)-adic numbers?

To prove that two specific rotations generate a free group, we can use the \(p\)-adic action!

If a group acts on a tree and two elements have no common fixed point, then they generate a free group.

Rational rotations act on \(p\)-adic trees, and the tree structure makes it easy to check freeness. The "hidden geometry" from the prime numbers provides the scaffolding for the paradox!
