Introduction

A Theory of Harmony

Why does Western music use the major scale? Is it arbitrary — a cultural accident — or is there something inevitable about it?

Can we deduce the major scale from basic physics and a couple of aesthetic values?

We'll start from two empirical observations about how sound works, add two values about what we want from music, and watch the major scale emerge as the optimal solution.

Observation 1

Two sine waves at nearby frequencies produce an unpleasant beating — they sound dissonant.

Observation 2

A vibrating string doesn't vibrate as a simple sine wave — it produces a rich spectrum of overtones at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.

Value 1

We want notes that sound good together — harmony (chords).

Value 2

We want chords that sound good together — chord progressions.

Observation 1 · Beating

Nearby Frequencies Sound Dissonant

When two sine waves have nearly the same frequency, they alternately reinforce and cancel each other. You hear a pulsating "wah-wah-wah" called beating.

$\sin(2\pi f_1 t) + \sin(2\pi f_2 t) = 2\cos\!\bigl(\pi(f_1 - f_2)t\bigr)\,\sin\!\bigl(\pi(f_1 + f_2)t\bigr)$

The envelope oscillates at the difference frequency $|f_1 - f_2|$. When this is in the range of roughly 20–50 Hz, the result is perceived as rough and unpleasant.

Key insight: Dissonance isn't about absolute pitch — it's about proximity. Two sine waves are consonant when they're either identical or far enough apart. The "danger zone" is the critical bandwidth.

Observation 2 · Overtones

The Harmonic Series

A string fixed at both ends doesn't vibrate in just one simple pattern. It simultaneously vibrates in all possible standing wave modes:

$y(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\cos(2\pi n f_0 t)$

The frequencies are integer multiples of the fundamental $f_0$: the harmonic series.

  1. $f_0$ — the fundamental (the note you "hear")
  2. $2f_0$ — one octave above
  3. $3f_0$ — an octave + a fifth (the "twelfth")
  4. $4f_0$ — two octaves above
  5. $5f_0$ — two octaves + a major third
  6. $6f_0$ — two octaves + a fifth

Value 1 · Consonance

Notes That Sound Good Together

Given Observation 1 (nearby frequencies beat) and Observation 2 (real tones have overtones), when do two notes not clash?

Two notes sound consonant when their overtones align rather than sitting in each other's critical bandwidths. This happens when the frequency ratio is a simple fraction:

Value 2 · Progression

Chords That Sound Good Together

Now we want more than just pairs of notes — we want chords (triads), and we want multiple chords that share notes so we can move smoothly between them.

The most consonant triad is the major triad: ratios 4:5:6. Starting from a root, we take a major third (5/4) and a perfect fifth (3/2).

To build chord progressions, we want several major (or minor) triads that all use notes from the same scale. Let's build triads on the 1st, 4th, and 5th scale degrees:

The major scale is the union of three major triads built on notes related by fifths. $C = \{C,E,G\}$, $F = \{F,A,C\}$, $G = \{G,B,D\}$ → $\{C,D,E,F,G,A,B\}$.

Conclusion

The Major Scale Emerges

Let's retrace the logic:

Physics

Nearby sine waves beat (dissonance), and real tones have overtones at integer multiples. Therefore, consonant intervals have simple frequency ratios.

Aesthetic

We want consonant chords (triads with simple ratios = major triad 4:5:6), and we want several such chords sharing a common pool of notes (chord progressions).

The simplest way to get multiple major triads is to stack them by fifths: take a root chord (I), go up a fifth (V), and down a fifth (IV). The union of these three triads gives exactly seven notes.

$\{C,E,G\} \cup \{G,B,D\} \cup \{F,A,C\} = \{C,D,E,F,G,A,B\}$

That's the major scale. It isn't an arbitrary choice — it's the most harmonious 7-note scale, deduced from basic physics and the desire for rich harmonic relationships.

The major scale is not a cultural accident — it is the optimal solution to the problem of finding notes that sound good together, with chords that sound good together.
